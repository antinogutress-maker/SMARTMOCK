Animated Pipe Fittings Guide

Visualize the mechanics of water supply fittings. Seeing how the flow changes and how parts connect will cement these definitions for your Loksewa exams.

Sockets (Couplings)

Used to join two pipes of the same diameter in a straight line. Notice how the pipes slide into the single, wider cylindrical sleeve to create a continuous straight flow.

Elbows (90° and 45°)

Used to change the direction of the pipeline to follow the terrain. The animation shows a 90° elbow smoothly redirecting the water flow vertically to horizontally.

Tee (Equal & Reducing)

Used to take a branch line off the main line. A "Reducing Tee" is used if the branch pipe is smaller than the main. Watch how the main flow splits into two separate directions.

Reducers (Concentric & Eccentric)

Used to connect a larger diameter pipe to a smaller one. As water enters the narrower section, its velocity increases (shown by faster flow lines on the right).

Unions

Installed at intervals to allow a section of the pipe to be easily removed or replaced without cutting the whole line. The animation shows the central nut unscrewing, allowing the right pipe segment to drop away freely.

Nipples

Short lengths of pipe (less than 30 cm) threaded at both ends, used to connect two female fittings together. Shown here bridging the gap between two larger fittings.

Plugs and Caps

Used to temporarily or permanently close the end of a pipe. The animation shows water flowing to the end of the line and being completely stopped by the end cap.

Flanges

Used for joining large diameter pipes or connecting pipes to heavy valves/pumps using bolts and gaskets. Watch the two flat plates slide together and bolt tightly to create a high-pressure seal.
