Darcy-Weisbach Equation

Interactive Derivation for Pipe Friction Head Loss

1. Fundamental Assumptions

  • The pipe is running completely full of fluid.
  • The flow is steady and uniform (velocity and cross-sectional area are constant along the length).
  • The pipe is strictly horizontal (elevation head $Z_1 = Z_2$).
  • The fluid is incompressible (constant density, $\rho$).

3. Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation

Step A: Balancing the Forces
Since the flow is uniform (no acceleration), the net force acting on the fluid cylinder is zero. The driving pressure force is balanced by the opposing pressure and the resisting frictional shear force on the wetted perimeter.

$$P_1 \cdot A - P_2 \cdot A - \tau_0 \cdot (\text{Wetted Area}) = 0$$ $$(P_1 - P_2) \cdot A = \tau_0 \cdot (\pi \cdot D \cdot L)$$

Substituting the cross-sectional area $A = \frac{\pi \cdot D^2}{4}$:

$$(P_1 - P_2) \cdot \frac{\pi \cdot D^2}{4} = \tau_0 \cdot \pi \cdot D \cdot L$$ $$P_1 - P_2 = \frac{4 \cdot \tau_0 \cdot L}{D}$$

Step B: Converting Pressure Difference to Head Loss
The drop in pressure is responsible for the frictional head loss ($h_f$). Dividing the pressure difference by the specific weight of the fluid ($\gamma = \rho g$):

$$h_f = \frac{P_1 - P_2}{\rho \cdot g}$$ $$h_f = \frac{4 \cdot \tau_0 \cdot L}{\rho \cdot g \cdot D}$$

Step C: Introducing the Coefficient of Friction
Froude experimentally deduced that frictional shear stress ($\tau_0$) is proportional to the square of the velocity ($V^2$). It is expressed as $\tau_0 = f' \cdot \frac{\rho \cdot V^2}{2}$, where $f'$ is the coefficient of friction.

$$h_f = \frac{4 \cdot \left(f' \cdot \frac{\rho \cdot V^2}{2}\right) \cdot L}{\rho \cdot g \cdot D}$$ $$h_f = \frac{4 \cdot f' \cdot L \cdot V^2}{2 \cdot g \cdot D}$$

Step D: The Final Darcy-Weisbach Form
To simplify, modern fluid mechanics replaces the term $4f'$ with a single variable $f$, which is known as the Darcy Friction Factor ($f = 4f'$).

$$h_f = \frac{f \cdot L \cdot V^2}{2 \cdot g \cdot D}$$
Professor's Golden Rule for Exams:
Always verify if the question gives you the "Friction Factor" ($f$) or the "Coefficient of Friction" ($f'$). If the question provides the Coefficient of Friction (e.g., $f' = 0.005$), you must multiply it by 4 before using the standard equation ($f = 4 \times 0.005 = 0.02$).
