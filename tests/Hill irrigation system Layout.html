Hill Irrigation System

Comprehensive Layout Plan & Major Engineering Challenges

1

Layout Plan of Hill Irrigation System & Associated Structures

10 Marks

Sequential Flow & Structures

  • 1. River/Khola (Source): The natural mountain stream acting as the perennial or seasonal water source.
  • 2. Headworks (Intake): Usually a side intake or bottom-trench weir built at a stable bend to divert water while keeping out large boulders.
  • 3. De-silting Basin: A widened canal section right after the intake. It reduces velocity to settle and flush out heavy suspended silt before it enters the main canal.
  • 4. Contour Canal: The main canal aligned along the natural hill contours to maintain a gentle, non-scouring gradient.

Crossing & Distribution

  • 5. Cross Drainage (CD) Works: Structures like Aqueducts or Super Passages built where the contour canal intersects natural gullies or rain-fed streams.
  • 6. Canal Fall (Drop): Constructed when the natural ground slope is too steep. It lowers the water safely to avoid high-velocity scouring.
  • 7. Protection Works: Breast walls on the uphill side and Retaining walls on the downhill side to stabilize the canal bench.
  • 8. Division Box & Outlets: Distributes the water from the main canal into smaller field channels feeding the terraced agricultural command area.
2

Major Problems in Hill Irrigation Systems

5 Marks
  • Topographical & Geological Instability: Hilly terrains are highly prone to landslides, rockfalls, and slope failures. These natural hazards frequently bury, breach, or wash away entire sections of the contour canal.
  • High Flow Velocities & Severe Scouring: Because of the steep terrain, maintaining a gentle canal bed slope is difficult. High-velocity flows cause severe scouring of the canal bed and banks, requiring frequent maintenance and solid masonry drops.
  • Heavy Sediment & Bed Load: During the monsoon, mountain streams (Kholas) carry massive amounts of gravel, boulders, and silt. This heavy bed load frequently damages headworks, chokes intakes, and silts up the canals, demanding rigorous de-silting operations.
  • Excessive Seepage Losses: The soils in hilly regions are often highly permeable (gravelly or fractured rocks). Earthen canals suffer from massive transit water losses through seepage, which can also trigger downhill landslides.
  • Flash Floods: The catchments in hills are small and steep, leading to sudden, intense flash floods during heavy rainfall. These floods can instantly destroy diversion weirs, CD works, and unprotected riverbanks.
  • Logistical & Maintenance Difficulties: The lack of motorable access roads makes transporting construction materials (cement, steel) extremely expensive and difficult. Routine maintenance and rapid emergency repairs are severely hindered by the rugged topography.
