Gravity Water Supply System Layout & Principles

A Gravity-Fed Water Supply System is an engineered infrastructure designed to convey water from an elevated source (such as a mountain spring or stream) to a lower-elevation community. Its defining characteristic is the exclusive reliance on the Earth's gravitational pull to provide the motive force, entirely eliminating the need for electro-mechanical pumping stations. This makes it highly reliable and economically sustainable in hilly terrains.

2. Primary Structural Components (6 Marks)

  • 1. Catchment & Intake: A heavily protected concrete structure constructed directly at the spring eye. It captures the aquifer yield while preventing surface contamination and large debris entry via iron trash racks.
  • 2. Sedimentation Tank: Positioned shortly after the intake, this basin slows the water's horizontal velocity (vh) to allow suspended solids (silt, sand) to settle out by gravity. Settling velocity (vs) is governed by Stokes' Law.
  • 3. Transmission Main: The primary conduit transporting raw water from the sedimentation tank down the mountain. This pipeline must withstand the highest static pressures.
  • 4. Interruption Chamber (IC): (Critical in Hilly Terrain) When the vertical drop creates a hydrostatic pressure that exceeds the safe working pressure of the pipes (e.g., > 60m head), a IC is installed. Water freely discharges into this open tank, effectively resetting the hydrostatic head back to zero (atmospheric pressure) before continuing its descent.
  • 5. Service Reservoir (SR): A balancing tank, typically cylindrical with a semicircular dome, situated just above the village. Springs provide a low, continuous yield over 24 hours, whereas community demand peaks in the morning and evening. The SR stores water during off-peak hours to satisfy peak flow demands.
  • 6 & 7. Distribution Network & Standposts: A branching network of smaller diameter pipes terminating at community standposts (taps) where households draw water. Gate valves strategically placed allow operators to isolate branches for maintenance.

3. Conclusion & System Efficiency (2 Marks)

In a fully operational state (100% automatic flow), dynamic equilibrium is achieved where the Source Yield effectively matches the Distribution Flow, maintaining a steady level within the Service Reservoir. The integration of ICs ensures catastrophic pipe failures are averted, rendering the gravity-fed model the apex standard for rural water engineering in mountainous topography.
