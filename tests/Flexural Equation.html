Bending Equation Derivation (Detailed Notes)

Derivation of the Flexural Formula

1. Fundamental Assumptions

Before proceeding with the mathematical derivation, the following assumptions must be strictly established. These dictate the limits of our formula.

  • The material of the beam is homogeneous (uniform physical properties throughout) and isotropic (elastic properties are the same in all directions).
  • The beam is initially straight and possesses a uniform cross-section.
  • Transverse sections which are plane before bending remain plane after bending (Bernoulli's assumption). This implies strain is directly proportional to the distance from the Neutral Axis.
  • The material obeys Hooke's Law ($\sigma \propto \epsilon$) and is stressed within the elastic limit.
  • The Modulus of Elasticity ($E$) is identical in both tension and compression.
  • The beam is subjected to pure bending, meaning the shear force is zero across the section being analyzed.

2. Part 1: Relationship between Strain, Stress, and Radius

Consider a small segment of the beam of length $dx$. Upon the application of a bending moment, this segment deforms into a circular arc with a center of curvature $O$ and a radius of curvature $R$.

Fig 1: Longitudinal geometry of the bent beam segment.

1. Original Length ($L$): The Neutral Axis (N.A.) neither elongates nor compresses. Its length is defined by the arc formula (Radius × Angle).
$$ L = R\theta $$

2. Extended Length ($L'$): Consider a longitudinal fiber located at a distance $y$ from the Neutral Axis. Its new radius is $(R+y)$.
$$ L' = (R + y)\theta $$

3. Strain ($\epsilon$): Engineering strain is the change in length divided by the original length.
$$ \epsilon = \frac{L' - L}{L} = \frac{(R + y)\theta - R\theta}{R\theta} = \frac{y\theta}{R\theta} $$

$$ \epsilon = \frac{y}{R} $$

4. Stress ($\sigma$): By Hooke's Law within the elastic limit, Stress equals Young's Modulus ($E$) times Strain.
$$ \sigma = E \cdot \epsilon $$ Substituting the value of $\epsilon$ derived above: $$ \sigma = E \cdot \left(\frac{y}{R}\right) $$

$$ \frac{\sigma}{y} = \frac{E}{R} \quad \dots \text{(Equation i)} $$

3. Part 2: Relationship between Moment and Radius

For the beam to remain in static equilibrium, the internal resisting moment created by the material's stress must perfectly balance the external bending moment ($M$) applied to the section.

Fig 2: Cross-section showing elemental area dA.

1. Elemental Force ($dF$): Consider an infinitesimally small area $dA$ at distance $y$ from the N.A. The force on this area is stress times area.
$$ dF = \sigma \cdot dA $$

2. Elemental Moment ($dM$): The moment of this internal force about the Neutral Axis is Force × perpendicular distance.
$$ dM = dF \cdot y = (\sigma \cdot dA) \cdot y $$

3. Substitution: From Equation (i), we know that $\sigma = \frac{E}{R} \cdot y$. Substitute this into the moment equation:
$$ dM = \left( \frac{E}{R} \cdot y \right) \cdot dA \cdot y $$ $$ dM = \frac{E}{R} \cdot y^2 \cdot dA $$

4. Total Bending Moment ($M$): To find the total internal resisting moment, we integrate $dM$ over the entire cross-sectional area. Because $E$ (material property) and $R$ (radius of curvature) are constant for a specific cross-section, they can be pulled outside the integral.

$$ M = \frac{E}{R} \int y^2 \, dA $$

5. Moment of Inertia ($I$): In mechanics, the integral term $\int y^2 \, dA$ is the standard definition for the Second Moment of Area, commonly known as the Moment of Inertia ($I$) of the section about the Neutral Axis.
$$ M = \frac{E}{R} \cdot I $$ Rearranging this establishes the second half of our relationship:

$$ \frac{M}{I} = \frac{E}{R} \quad \dots \text{(Equation ii)} $$

4. Final Combined Equation

By logically combining the findings from geometric strain (Equation i) and static equilibrium (Equation ii), we arrive at the complete, detailed derivation of the Flexural Formula:

$$ \frac{M}{I} = \frac{\sigma}{y} = \frac{E}{R} $$
