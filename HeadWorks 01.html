Interactive Simulation & Technical Layout

1. Weir or Barrage

  • Function: Raises the upstream river water level to create sufficient pressure (head) so water flows into the canal by gravity.
  • Main Feature: A barrage uses adjustable gates across its width for precise water level control, while a weir is primarily a solid, raised concrete or masonry wall.

2. Divide Wall

  • Function: Separates the turbulent water flowing over the main barrage from the calm, still water in the "pocket" directly in front of the canal, allowing heavy silt to settle.
  • Main Feature: A solid wall built perpendicular to the barrage axis, extending upstream into the river.

3. Under Sluices (Scouring Sluices)

  • Function: Periodically scours and flushes the accumulated silt from the quiet pocket back into the downstream river.
  • Main Feature: Gate-controlled openings adjacent to the divide wall. The crest level is lower than the main barrage to enable bottom-scouring.

4. Canal Head Regulator

  • Function: Regulates the exact volume of water entering the offtaking canal and completely shuts off flow during severe floods.
  • Main Feature: Built at the immediate entrance of the canal. Its sill level is raised higher than the under sluices so only the cleaner top layer of water enters.

5. Fish Ladder

  • Function: Ensures the safe migration of fish upstream and downstream past the barrage wall.
  • Main Feature: A narrow, sloping channel with staggered baffles to reduce water velocity.

6. Silt Excluder

  • Function: Prevents heavy, bottom-rolling sediment from ever entering the canal.
  • Main Feature: Built in the river bed directly in front of the Head Regulator. Parallel tunnels direct highly-silted bottom water through the under sluices, while clean top water enters the canal.

7. Silt Ejector (Silt Extractor)

  • Function: Removes finer suspended silt that managed to bypass the excluder and enter the canal.
  • Main Feature: Built across the bed of the canal, downstream from the Head Regulator. Curved tunnels draw out the bottom silty layer and discharge it into an escape channel back to the river.

Technical Comparison: Weir vs. Barrage

S.N. Feature Weir Barrage
1 Ponding Achievement Ponding is achieved mainly by a fixed, solid crest obstruction. Ponding is achieved almost entirely by adjustable gates.
2 Control Gates Consists of no gates or very small shutters on the crest. Consists of a series of large, adjustable mechanical gates.
3 Cost of Construction Relatively low cost; simpler design and less mechanical parts. High cost; involves complex gate systems and heavy foundations.
4 Roadway Connectivity Generally cannot be connected with a roadway for heavy traffic. Can be easily connected with a roadway (bridge) over the piers.
5 Crest and Gates High crest with very low (or no) shutters. Low crest with large (high) gates for flow regulation.
6 Silt Regulation Poor capacity to scour out silt; leads to higher deposition. Excellent scouring capacity through the use of under-sluices.
7 Flood Discharge Excess water flows over the crest; less control during floods. Gates can be lifted fully to pass high floods with minimal resistance.
8 Afflux (Rise in Water) Causes a high afflux during high floods due to the solid crest. Produces a very low afflux as gates are lifted during floods.
